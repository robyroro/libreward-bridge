ABSTRACT

LibreReward Bridge is AGPL-licensed middleware that lets a free/open-source service issue an exact-value, single-use GNU Taler reward without creating a recipient account or collecting the recipient's name, email, advertising identifier or browser fingerprint. An integrator creates a reward through an idempotent API, gives the recipient a short-lived bearer claim link, and receives signed lifecycle webhooks. An operator-controlled Taler wallet funds the peer-push claim.

A public 0.1 sandbox prototype already demonstrates the concept and is prior, unfunded work. The proposed project will not charge NLnet for that baseline. The new R&D will: (1) design and upstream a caller-idempotent, supported server-side peer-push operation; (2) replace the current testing-only wallet boundary with a capability-negotiated and documented operator interface; (3) publish a reusable integration kit and an AGPL Formbricks Community reference adapter for rewarding completed surveys without sharing response content or respondent identity with LibreReward; (4) deliver internationalised, WCAG 2.2 AA-tested claim flows, reproducible releases and operator documentation; and (5) complete an independent security review, remediation and a 1.0 release.

The result will be a generic technology commons for privacy-preserving rewards, research participation payments, community incentives, bug bounties and other small disbursements. It is an outbound reward capability, not a merchant checkout, exchange, bank, recipient wallet or payment institution.

The project has a clear European dimension independently of applicant location. It contributes directly to GNU Taler, a European-led open payment effort funded through Horizon Europe; targets self-hosted FOSS used by European research, civic and community organisations; and will demonstrate integration with Formbricks Community, whose maintainer organisation is based in Germany. The work advances European payment sovereignty and GDPR-aligned data minimisation by making a European libre payment stack easier to adopt without a proprietary payment processor or recipient-identity database. COMPARISON WITH EXISTING AND HISTORICAL EFFORTS

GNU Taler's merchant stack and the funded Taler Fastify plugin focus on accepting customer payments or donations. LibreReward moves in the opposite direction: an operator issues a bounded, single-use reward to a recipient.

The Taler Wallet ID Lookup Service supports identity-addressed P2P payments. LibreReward deliberately avoids a recipient directory or wallet address; possession of a short-lived claim capability is sufficient.

Taler in Liberapay adds Taler to a specific recurring-donation platform. The Interledger interoperability inquiry studies protocol interoperability for web monetisation. LibreReward instead provides a deployable, platform-neutral reward lifecycle with creation idempotency, claim races, reconciliation, tenant isolation and signed result webhooks.

The proposed Formbricks adapter is a concrete adoption example, not a dependency of the bridge. Unlike a conventional survey incentive system, the bridge does not need survey answers or respondent identity. The proposal must preserve this distinction and avoid duplicating inbound checkout work already funded by NGI TALER.

SIGNIFICANT TECHNICAL CHALLENGES

1. An external wallet operation can succeed while the caller observes a timeout. Without a caller reference or authoritative lookup, automatically repeating peer-push initiation can create duplicate liabilities. The project will solve this upstream where possible and fail safely where it cannot.
2. Wallet-core is primarily a user-wallet component. A server operator needs an authenticated, serialized and supportable boundary with explicit version and state semantics.
3. Bearer claim URLs and taler://pay-push URIs must remain usable while resisting leakage, replay, logging and cross-tenant access.
4. Integrators need a simple lifecycle without weakening financial correctness. Database state, wallet state and webhook delivery must remain reconcilable across crashes and retries.
5. Real deployments encounter exchange terms, KYC thresholds, liquidity and accounting policies. The software must expose these states honestly without becoming an exchange or pretending to settle jurisdiction-specific legal questions.

ECOSYSTEM, ENGAGEMENT AND PROMOTION

All results will be developed in public under AGPL-3.0-or-later. The project will use the GNU Taler mailing list, bug tracker and Integration Community Hub for protocol/interface work. Design proposals and patches will be sent upstream early, with public links recorded in the repository.

For adoption, the project will contact the Formbricks community about the reference adapter and invite other FOSS survey, research, community and bug-bounty projects to test the generic API. Feedback and decisions will be recorded without personal or confidential payment data.

A public status page will be updated at least every two months. Each milestone will publish code, tests, documentation and a short demonstration or report. Releases, SBOMs, audit dispositions and migration notes will remain available without login. Sustainability comes from a small protocol-independent core, a stable OpenAPI contract, copyleft licensing, documented self-hosting and upstreaming changes rather than maintaining a permanent private fork. A future hosted service may support maintenance, but use of the software will never depend on that service.
